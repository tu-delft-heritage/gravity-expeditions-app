Even though the atmosphere aboard the K-XVIII was presented to be excellent in the movie and books, the reality turned out to be different. There were tensions between the professor and lieutenant commander Hetterschij and lieutenant Wytema<sup>i</sup>. The officers were primarily engaged with waving the flag in the foreign countries. They tried to make the submarine look at its best throughout the journey. Vening Meinesz wanted to perform as many diving procedures as possible to perform his measurements. These two goals did not always go hand in hand. The submarine had some technical defects during the last part of the expedition and Hetterschij wanted to arrive in Surabaya with the submarine in good condition. Hetterschij thought diving procedures would damage the submarine further, so he decided that no diving procedures were to be performed during the route across the Indian Ocean<sup>ii</sup>. However, this part of the journey was very important to the professor.

Another problem aboard the K-XVIII was Vening Meinesz’s correspondence with the minister of defence, L.N. Deckers. Lieutenant commander Hetterschij thought the professor was complaining about him, but the professor claimed he was just writing about his scientific work. Vening Meinesz got the feeling that Hetterschij provoked other officers to think badly of him<sup>iii</sup>. The relationship between Vening Meinesz and Wytema was damaged during this expedition. They had travelled together aboard the K-XIII and their relationship running up to this journey had been amicable. Their correspondence after the K-XVIII was much more detached<sup>iv</sup>. None of these tensions were brought to the public, because one of the expedition’s goals was to improve the reputation of the Royal Netherlands Navy. Neither the professor, nor the officers wanted to damage this new reputation<sup>v</sup>.
